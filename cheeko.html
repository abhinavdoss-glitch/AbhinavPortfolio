03 — The Brief That Lied

Two completely different
humans. One tiny object.

The brief said: "build a toy for kids aged 2–10." Simple enough. But when you pull the thread, the real brief reveals itself — convince a 4-year-old this is more fun than a tablet. Convince her mother it's safer than silence. At the same time. With the same 15-centimetre object. No screen. No app. No compromise on either side.

That's not a product brief. That's a negotiation between two entirely different nervous systems — one driven by wonder, one driven by worry. And the only thing both trust equally is how something feels in the hand.

What the child needed to feel
A best friend who knows everything. Answers questions about dinosaurs at 7am. Sings songs. Tells stories. Never says "not now." Always on her side.
What the parent needed to feel
Complete control without being controlling. A window into their child's world — not a surveillance app, but a reassurance layer that whispers: everything is fine.
We walked into a market that didn't exist. Not one AI toy in India spoke Hindi. Not one gave parents visibility. Not one looked like it cost more than it was worth. We weren't competing with other toys — we were competing with the screen itself.

04 — The Real Problem

Four gaps. Zero products
filling any of them.

Before we designed a single pixel, we mapped every failure point in the market. Four structural problems no one had solved — not Amazon, not LeapFrog, not anyone operating in India.

01
No safe, screen-free premium alternative
The only screen-free options were plastic xylophones and sticker books. Nothing AI-powered. Nothing that grew with the child. The premium gap was enormous and invisible.
02
Every AI toy spoke English. Only English.
In a country with 22 official languages and 1.4 billion people, every AI toy available assumed English as the first language. We called it what it was: a design failure at national scale.
03
Parents were flying blind
When a child is talking to an AI, what is the AI saying back? Parents had no way to know. No logs, no summaries, no alerts. The trust gap wasn't a feature request — it was blocking sales.
04
"No screen" had to feel like a luxury, not a punishment
Every parent knows screens are a problem. But removing them feels like taking something away. The design had to make absence feel intentional — like a choice a smart parent makes, not a compromise.
05 — Design Iterations

Five versions to get it right

V1Ages 2–4
Cheeko Classic
Rounded form, single large button, no display. Philosophy established: touch should be the only interface. Warmth built through texture and sound, not visuals.
V2Ages 4–10
Cheeko Pro
Small pixel display for expressions only — not content. NFC cards for story selection. Screen-free philosophy maintained while adding visual personality.
V3Post-Testing
Form Refinements
Handle height reduced after ergonomic testing. Button texture changed to ribbed surface for tactile feedback. Every millimeter tested with actual 4-year-olds.
V4Acoustic + Visual
Speaker Grille Design
Speaker grille redesigned to read as a smile from a distance. The product literally smiles at you — acoustic function became emotional communication.
V5Live in Market
Launch-Ready
Validated through 3 rounds of focus groups. Final form earned 4★ on App Store at launch. The toy that shipped had the confidence of everything that didn't.

07 — The Campaign That Sold Out a Launch

We didn't sell a toy.
We sold a parenting decision.

The child doesn't buy Cheeko. The parent does. So every piece of social content was engineered around a single insight: Indian parents in the 28–40 bracket are silently exhausted by the question-and-screen cycle — and they feel guilty about both. We made Cheeko the answer to that guilt. Not "a fun toy." A smarter choice.

Three post archetypes. All launched within 48 hours of each other. All told the same story from three different angles. The result: 10 orders before the week was out.

09 — The Decisions That Defined It

Every decision was a trade-off

Senior design isn't making every right call. It's knowing which wrong calls you can live with — and being honest about why you made them. These are the four that shaped Cheeko more than anything else.

01
01 — Philosophy
No screen as design philosophy
Made absence feel premium, not limited. Every curve was a trust signal — the product had to communicate safety through form alone.
↯ Trade-off: interaction richness vs philosophical coherence
02
02 — Campaign
AI-generated campaign at launch
Kling video, Gemini Gems for brand workflows. India's first fully AI-generated toy campaign at launch scale.
↯ Trade-off: photographic authenticity vs creative velocity
03
03 — Launch
Synchronized launch pressure cooker
Page + social + influencer all activated simultaneously. Manufactured momentum on day one. No phased rollout.
↯ Trade-off: upfront marketing intensity vs organic growth
04
04 — Architecture
Proposed parent company: Altio AI
Established Altio AI as holding entity for multiple AI products. Cheeko was the first — not the last. Scalability baked in from the start.
↯ Trade-off: structural complexity now vs platform scale later
